<h3>
    Intro:
</h3>
<p class="postParagraph">
    Over the past semester (Fall 2021, senior undergraduate year), I collected a large quantity of data pertaining to my productivity. This is not my first experience with constructing productivity metrics, but instead a massive redesign of my previous system.
    Unfortunately, with the revamping of my metrics, the data is no longer backwards compatible, so the two year backlog of data will have to be analyzed another time. Nevertheless, this blog post will be overviewing the results of my semester, determining
    if there are any significant trends, and examining the sub factors that contributed most to my overall productivity.
</p>
<p class="postParagraph">
    The purpose of this project is multivariate and can be categorized as evaluating the efficacy of my productivity system as a metric; learning about plotly, pandas, and jupyter; and, making a cool project for my website. I also was inspired by various
    “data journalism” articles made by NYT and WaPo, and hopefully, I can emulate their ability to tell a story through pretty data visualizations.
</p>

</br>

<h3>
    Methodology:
</h3>
<p class="postParagraph">
    I constructed my productivity metric based on 7 primary factors: deep work, moderate work, light work, reading, meditation, sleep, and exercise. I picked these categories because I believe that they occupy fairly distinct spaces of productivity and are
    of high personal importance (I want to accurately measure them so I can quantifiably improve them later) -- I will expand what kind of work qualifies in each category in several paragraphs.
</p>
<p class="postParagraph">
    Each factor is either binary, or quantifiable. Binary factors can only be satisfied once a day, while quantifiable factors are measured cyclically. The cycle length is a uniform 25 minutes for each category with the exception of the “meditation” category,
    which is 10 minutes long. The justification behind the length was simple, 25 minutes is the length of a pomodoro cycle, and 10 minutes is the length of a guided meditation session in the Waking Up app. These cycles make up the fundamental unit of
    productivity, and upon completion of a cycle, the day is awarded a certain amount of productivity points determined by the productivity weight (outlined in the table below). The sum of all these points per day makeup the productivity score of the
    day. These points vary between the categories because certain forms of productivity are more valuable than others -- per minute of productivity, the“Meditation” category is the most valuable category. The weight characteristic is also a useful incentive
    lever, as I am able to incentivize certain kinds of productivity over others by increasing and decreasing their respective weights.
</p>

</i>
</br>
<p class="postParagraph">
    Furthermore, each productivity factor has an ideal amount of cycles per day. If each factor is performed the ideal amount of times per day, i.e 6 cycles of “Moderate Work” per day, then the total productivity score of the day would reach 100, this is
    because the product of all weights and cycles would summate to 100. This was actually the way I derived the weights for each category. I decided what an ideal day looked like, and how many cycles of each productivity category such a day would have,
    then I manipulated the weights by the importance of the productivity factor and so that the sum of the weights and cycles would equal to 100. The table above demonstrates the result.
</p>

<p class="postParagraph">
    Obviously, keeping track of my progress day to day would be very tedious if not automated tools. Because of this, I have utilized “Notion” for the automated calculation, storing, and displaying of my productivity. Because of this, collecting productivity
    data has been surprisingly frictionless, and it has quickly become a habit. I spend no more than a total of 10 minutes a day managing the collection of data, and it literally has never been annoying. Also, out of convenience, I do not always utilize
    Pomodoro timers for collecting the amount of cycles of productivity a certain task has taken. More often than not, I simply use a simple timer to collect the amount of minutes elapsed, divide the number of minutes in a cycle, and round down the cycle
    count to a round integer.
</p>
<code>
    round(prop("Deep Work (6 Cycles)") * 36 / 6 + prop("Moderate Work (6 cycles)") * 24 / 6 + prop("Light Work (4 Cycles)") * 8 / 4 + prop("Reading (2 Cycles)") * 10 / 2 + toNumber(prop("8:15 Sleep")) * 8 + prop("Meditate (20)") / 2 * 6 + toNumber(prop("Workout (2 Cycles)")) * 8)
</code>

    can tell because of the lack of high score days forming a diagonal cluster when looking top-down at the graph). Another interesting thing is that the data points form a hyperplane -- the data points forming a 2 dimensional surface within a three dimensional

<h3>
    Conclusion:
</h3>

<p class="postParagraph">
    I would consider the productivity metric project a success, the metric seems to be quite accurate. Days that seemed unproductive scored low on the metric, and the days that were productive scored high. The cluster of days around midterms/finals had high
    scores, while the areas in between exams had lower scores. Unfortunately, I can not think of any method to validate these intuitions, but I do not see any obvious flaws with this system. Overall, I believe that the productivity factor computed through
    the number of cycles and their respective weights closely approximates my real productivity.

</p>

<p class="postParagraph">
    Although I did not have an explicit goal of improving my productivity over the course of the semester, I was still disappointed to see no overall change. Whatever gains I made in one productivity category led to the decrease of productivity in other sectors,
    reducing the diversity of my days. Because of this, next semester, I hope to maintain an overall semester productivity score of 70 without sacrificing productivity diversity, and more importantly, maintaining a statistically significant positive slope
    over the course of the semester.
</p>

<p class="postParagraph">
    I am also painfully aware of Goodhart’s law -- when any measurement becomes a target, it ceases to become a good measurement. This usually happens because of misaligned incentives between the people administrating the metric/target and the people’s performance
    that is being counted. Unfortunately, a similar problem arises in my personal case. My only guard against this issue is personal honesty and integrity, I always make sure to be very conservative with what counts as work, and whenever I have doubts
    about
</p>

<h4> Couple Observations:</h4>
<ol>
    <li>The productivity system made it feel more rewarding to work. Even if I was being unsuccessful in whatever endeavor I was doing, it was still being counted towards something. Also achieving new daily productivity high scores during finals was incentivizing
        to study harder and longer.
    </li>
    <li>Surprisingly frictionless. I never found it burdensome to record productivity.
    </li>
    <li>I did not change weights during the course of the semester, the initial weight tuning at the start was surprisingly good.
    </li>
    <li>I would guess that this system makes me at least 3-5% happier. It gives me structure when nothing else can. It also validated the days I worked really hard, rewarding me with a high score at the end of the day.
    </li>
    <li>I am far from my ideal day, it may take several years of work to get there.
    </li>
</ol>

<h3>Future Improvements:</h3>
<p class="postParagraph">
    I do not think I will make any changes to the productivity system. This is for two reasons. First, systems like these are fragile, and attempting to extract too much information may lead to user friction and abandonment of the system altogether. Second
    of all, this would make all the long data parsing code incompatible, and that would be annoying.

</p>
<p class="postParagraph">
    I will probably buy an apple watch and develop a native app for this productivity system. This will automate the system further and make it even more frictionless. I will also auto upload the data to my website, this will boost accountability.

</p>
